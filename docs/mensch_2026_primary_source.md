# Mensch (2026) — primary-source notes

## Source

Mensch, A. (2026, May 12). *Audition devant la commission d'enquête sur les
dépendances structurelles et les vulnérabilités systémiques dans le secteur
du numérique et les risques pour l'indépendance de la France.* Assemblée
nationale, Paris.

A separate public hearing in the same parliamentary cycle is referenced
in the paper as:

> Assemblée nationale (2026). *Création, diffusion et acquisition des
> connaissances : comment l'intelligence artificielle transforme notre
> éducation et notre culture* (rapporteure Céline Calvez). Audition de
> Luc Ferry, Laurent Alexandre et Olivier Babeau, 8 avril 2026. XVIIe
> législature, transcription officielle.

The Mensch testimony is mobilized in the paper as a primary source
articulating, in contemporary policy register and from an operational
vantage, structural arguments that the framework develops in analytical
register. This document consolidates the structural arguments the
framework borrows from the testimony and the reconciliation of numerical
magnitudes flagged in Appendix H.4.

---

## Structural arguments mobilized

### A. Cloud and AI are operationally integrated (Section 4)

Mensch (2026) argues that contemporary digital services and artificial
intelligence are not analytically separable categories — *cloud is
artificial intelligence* in the sense that the same firms supply the
training compute (Layer 1), the foundation models (Layer 2), and the
inference capability access (Layer 3) as a single operational bundle.

The framework cites this directly in Section 4 to qualify the application
of the seven-layer decomposition: layers are kept analytically distinct
because their commoditization velocities differ in sign and magnitude,
but the same firms that benefit from the anti-commoditization of Layer 1
are also the firms that supply the Layer 3 access whose price is falling.
The dual position drives the strategic analysis of Section 8 and the
upstream-chain mapping of Appendix F.

### B. Sovereignty as leverage rather than isolation (Section 4.1)

Mensch (2026) reframes "sovereignty" in the digital and AI context not as
isolationism but as *leverage* — the capacity of a bloc to retain enough
of the value chain that it can negotiate terms with the blocs that supply
the parts it does not produce.

The framework adopts this framing in Section 4.1: K7 is a descriptive
parameter of the regime rather than a normative variable. A bloc that
imports the entirety of its frontier-model capability has no leverage in
negotiations with the supplying bloc, regardless of whether the
importing bloc declares its preferences in the language of sovereignty
or in the language of integration.

### C. Macroeconomic order of magnitude (Section 4.1)

Mensch (2026) places the prospective consumption of AI services by
European firms at approximately 10 percent of the European wage bill
within a three-to-four-year horizon, corresponding to approximately one
trillion euros annually.

The framework cites this figure as illustrative (not validated
independently) but uses the order of magnitude to argue that the
cumulative current-account effect of importing the entire AI-service
stack from a different bloc is comparable in magnitude to the value of
the labor activity that the imported services substitute. This is the
macro-level counterpart of the firm-level finding of Appendix E.2:
institution-dominant firms below the team-size threshold cannot
economically migrate; a bloc with insufficient upstream capability
cannot capture the value flow at the macroeconomic level.

### D. Temporal closure of upstream supply capacity (Section 8.4)

Mensch (2026) argues that the window during which alternative upstream
capacity can be built in a given bloc is itself a function of decisions
taken in other blocs: when a small number of actors with deep balance
sheets commit to deploying training and inference capacity at scale
ahead of demand, the energy, foundry, memory, and data-center resources
required to build alternative capacity in the same horizon are
progressively absorbed.

The framework articulates this as a temporal dimension of vendor
concentration risk (Section 8.4): vendor concentration is not solely a
property of the present configuration of suppliers but also a property
of the upstream conditions under which alternative suppliers might or
might not emerge in the relevant horizon. The framework does not
forecast the closure of any specific window, nor does it endorse the
specific magnitudes Mensch advances; what it articulates is the
structural mechanism.

### E. Cultural-political character of AI artifacts (Appendix G.3)

In testimony cited in the closing paragraph of Appendix G.3, the
frontier-model developer characterizes the models its industry produces
as themselves carrying a *politics* — shaping cultural representations,
languages, and the ethical postures embedded in the code they generate.

The framework cites this articulation as a contemporary policy
formulation of the question Winner (1980) posed philosophically: the
politics of artifacts is being formulated, in different vocabulary, by
the actors building the artifacts in question. Appendix G.3 develops
this dimension in the epistemic-justice register.

## Reconciliation of numerical magnitudes (Appendix H.4)

The specific numerical magnitudes the testimony advances reflect three
reporting choices the reader should note before citing them:

| Quantity | Mensch value | Reconciliation |
|---|---|---|
| European AI consumption (% of wage bill) | ≈10% | Compatible with a broad definition of Europe including EU-27, EFTA, and the UK. Under strict EU-27 the absolute figure is closer to €800 billion at 2025 data. |
| European AI consumption (absolute) | ≈€1 trillion / year | Same caveat as above. Order of magnitude. |
| Extra-European capex deployment | ≈US$1 trillion / year | Aggregates multiple announced commitments: Big Five hyperscaler 2026 capex ≈US$600–690B; Stargate Project ≈US$500B over four years; adjacent commitments. Rounding to the relevant order of magnitude. |
| Token-price reference (input) | ≈€1 / 1M tokens | Compatible with entry-tier European frontier-model catalogues and with the lowest-cost tier of US providers (Anthropic Haiku 4.5 at US$1 input). Diverges from dominant frontier-tier pricing (Sonnet 4.6 / Opus 4.7 at US$3–5 input). |
| Token-price reference (output) | ≈€3 / 1M tokens | Same caveat. Frontier-tier US output pricing is US$15–25 per million tokens. |

**Bottom line:** the structural arguments the framework mobilizes from
the Mensch testimony (operational integration of upstream layers,
sovereignty as leverage, temporal closure of upstream supply capacity,
political character of frontier models) are *preserved across plausible
recalibrations* of these numerical magnitudes. Practitioners citing the
testimony for the structural argument should be aware of the
reconciliation above and either cite the Mensch values directly with
explicit attribution or substitute their own calibration consistent with
their preferred geographical definition.
